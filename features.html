What’s Built

Not a wishlist. Every system below is implemented and running in-engine today — the screenshots are real captures, and the environment art is the game’s actual scenery.

01 / The Cast

250 Thinking Souls

Every one of ~250 townsfolk has a mind of its own.

Beaver Valley is populated by roughly 250 named characters, and almost every one thinks for itself. Each has a distinct personality, private goals, allegiances, and relationships — and reacts to where it is, what’s happening, and what you just did. The whole cast improvises in-character, every single time, instead of reading canned lines.

  • 100+ hand-written personalities across a roster of ~250 named characters.
  • Real memory of the moment — who’s nearby, what they’re doing, and what just happened feeds every reaction.
  • They never repeat themselves — even a character’s signature lines come out fresh.
  • Crowds get their own people too — background fighters roll a random name, temperament, and backstory on the spot.
  • Unfiltered and in-character — they talk like who they are, not like a help desk.
02 / Simulation

A Living Town That Runs Itself

Wander in and the streets are already alive — on a schedule, in conversation, and never the same twice.

The town keeps a daily clock, and everyone keeps a routine. Characters walk real routes between venues, settle into whatever they came to do, and gather for events. They strike up conversations with each other, react to violence and death around them, and — if you speak up — answer you out loud, in character. Because it all grows out of their own choices, no two visits play out the same way.

  • Different every single time — the day unfolds from the characters’ own decisions, so the town you walk into is never the town you left.
  • A daily routine for every soul — jobs, meals, the bar, church, the McDoogles lunch rush — that you can watch cross the streets.
  • They actually do things — sit, eat, drink, work out, dance, stand guard — instead of standing frozen.
  • Characters talk to each other unprompted, and rotate who’s talking so it never gets stale.
  • Witnesses react to a killing — they gather at the body, take it in, and the gossip changes.
  • Talk to the town — speak into your mic and the nearest character genuinely answers.
03 / Agency

Souls That Act

Characters don’t just talk — they reach into the world and do things.

A character’s decisions play out physically, through the same movement, combat and interaction systems you use. Deciding to grab a weapon, join your party, hand you an item, flee, or start a conversation with someone across the room all actually happen on screen — not as a menu, but as behavior.

  • Attack, block, pick up, throw, shoot, follow, retreat, give, search — and more — all decided by the character.
  • They understand what you mean — ask for “a boomstick” or “something to fight with” and they find it.
  • They’ll join — or turn on — you and coach you toward their goals, or their own.
  • Hidden secrets to uncover by searching the right place at the right time.
  • Nobody games the system — a defendant can’t just declare itself innocent, and a bystander can’t order the police.
04 / Voices

Everyone Speaks — In Their Own Voice

No scripted dialogue, and no two characters sound alike.

Characters don’t just generate words — they say them out loud, each in their own distinct, recognizable voice, with speech bubbles to match. Dozens of individual voices span the cast, from original creations to uncanny satirical impressions, and the conversation flows naturally from one speaker to the next.

  • Every character has its own voice — dozens of them, assigned per character.
  • 44 distinct voices just for the war-front soldiers, on top of the main cast.
  • Conversations stay in order and on time, so exchanges land like real back-and-forth.
  • Instant reactions cover the beat before a full reply lands, so nobody stands there mute.
  • You can talk back — the game listens to your actual voice.
05 / Performance

Live Music, Bands & Dance

Real songs, played by real bands, to crowds that actually move.

The Valley has a whole music scene. Bands take the stage and perform real tracks — drummers hit on the beat, guitarists strum and fret in time — while crowds and even marching soldiers break into synchronized dance routines and formations. Five rival acts, each with its own sound, fight over the town’s cultural soul.

  • Beaver Machine — the town’s house band, headlining the bar.
  • Stone City Orchestra — the old-world classical establishment.
  • Voluntary Mother Earth, E$$E M & Poppa Capp and D3@DH0R$3 — the counter-culture, the hip-hop underground, and the hyperpop.
  • CuntPuncher — the imported British rivals nobody asked for.
  • Performances play in real time to a moving, dancing crowd — not a looping backdrop.
06 / Crossover

A Crossover Cast & the Twitch Telethon

The characters come from a whole universe of shows — and your stream chat can join them.

Beaver Valley didn’t dream up its cast from nothing. Many of its characters are pulled from an existing body of work — the people from Dumpster Talk, Homeless in Denton, Put It In Your Mouth, and Namasensei’s Japanese Lessons all wound up living in the Valley. And in Telethon mode, the game plugs into a live Twitch stream so your own audience becomes cast and combatants.

  • Characters from Dumpster Talk — the talk show, which also gets filmed inside the game.
  • Characters from Homeless in Denton, the reality series.
  • Characters from Put It In Your Mouth, the cooking show.
  • Namasensei himself, from Namasensei’s Japanese Lessons, runs the dojo.
  • Live Twitch integration in Telethon mode turns your audience into participants, and the chaos survives scene changes.
07 / Justice

The Courtroom

A full trial where the jury genuinely decides — and the innocent can hang.

The courtroom is a complete trial from arraignment through opening statements, witness examination and cross, closings, deliberation, verdict and sentencing — all played out by characters speaking in their own voices. The jury votes for itself with no predetermined outcome, so verdicts aren’t bound to the truth. You can take any role — judge, defendant, prosecutor, defense, or witness — and even command the bailiff out loud.

  • Every phase of a real trial, start to finish, that always reaches a verdict.
  • A jury with a genuine opinion — the guilty walk free and the innocent get convicted.
  • Play any role by voice — a player judge issues spoken rulings and orders.
  • Group trials — get your whole party hauled in and sentenced one by one.
  • Set up your own case with any characters, saved and re-loadable.
08 / Justice

Police, Crime & Doing Time

Commit a crime in front of the wrong soul and the whole system comes for you.

Crimes aren’t magically known — a witness has to actually see one and go tell an officer. Police, led by Chief Jesse Ventura, investigate, escalate by how bad it was, cuff suspects, read them their rights, and march them off to booking. Offenders get scheduled for real trials you can attend, convicts serve time on a working cell block, and jailed characters can be broken out.

  • Crimes have to be witnessed and reported — from trespass all the way to murder.
  • Police that patrol, investigate, call backup, and arrest — giving orders out loud.
  • Getting cuffed is physical — and an ally can break you out, or you can pick the lock.
  • Get arrested yourself and it sticks with you across the whole town and into court.
  • A full jail day — chow, yard, visitation, lights-out — that you actually serve, or escape.
09 / Economy

The Beaver Valley Economy

Money is real, debt compounds, and the repo men will come for your stuff.

Every character carries a wallet and an inventory, and they earn, shop, eat and drink even when you’re not looking. There’s a full bank on top — savings, a vault, credit cards, cash advances, and a stock exchange. Fall behind on your bills and a persistent debt follows you: repo men turn up to seize your best belongings, and it escalates to a civil trial and jail.

  • A wallet and inventory on every soul — and their off-screen spending really moves the shops’ stock.
  • A real bank — savings, a protected vault, credit cards and cash advances.
  • A stock market of 19 in-world companies whose prices ride each business’s fortunes.
  • Debt has teeth — repo men, a “pay now or lose your goods” countdown, and a court summons.
  • A county hospital that heals wounds, replaces limbs, and bills you when you’re broke.
10 / Modes

Whole Games Within the Game

The same engine spins up a starship bridge, a battlefield, a dojo, and a fighting ring.

Beyond the town, the engine launches entire standalone modes. Command a starship bridge by voice while the hull tears open around you. Drop into four-player squad warfare across a dozen maps. Climb a martial-arts ladder in the dojo, or just throw any two characters into the ring and watch.

  • Starship Command — a five-role crew, orders given by voice, and a ship that comes apart under fire.
  • War — parachute in with up to 4 players, real squad tactics, cover, finite ammo, and 12 frontline maps.
  • Namasensei’s Dojo — a martial-arts campaign: fight bare-handed, earn nine belts, climb tournaments, and get back up when you lose a limb.
  • VS Arena — a no-stakes exhibition: pick any two of the cast, full weapons, health bars, last one standing.
  • Each mode is walled off from the others, so nothing bleeds where it shouldn’t.
11 / World

A Hand-Built World of 150+ Scenes

A grimy post-industrial town you can walk end to end — and it remembers what you left behind.

Beaver Valley spans over 150 hand-built scenes — city streets, slums, a sewer network beneath every district, a courthouse, jail, hospital wings, McDoogles, a bank, gun and sport shops, the TV studio, band houses, and an airport with cabin interiors. Walk through a doorway and the next place loads seamlessly while the last one waits exactly as you left it.

  • 150+ hand-authored scenes across 169 catalogued locations and 42 factions.
  • Seamless doorways — revisit anywhere and it’s instant, with everything right where it was.
  • Your world travels with you — party, weapons, ammo, prisoners and status all come along.
  • A true beat-em-up stage where everything sorts by its feet, scales with distance, and casts a shadow.
  • Built with a full editor suite — the town is authored, not procedurally spat out.
12 / Characters

The Roster & Customizer

One rig, ~250 characters — or build your own, limb by limb.

Every fighter, player or NPC, is built on one shared body so the whole cast moves consistently, and any of them can be your playable hero. Characters genuinely grip two-handed weapons with both hands. And a deep in-game customizer lets you edit every body part, with what you see in the editor matching exactly what shows up in the game.

  • ~250 playable characters on one shared, hand-tuned body.
  • Two-handed weapons are really held two-handed — hands, elbows and grip stay locked every frame.
  • A per-limb customizer with colors, and portable arm/leg “part sets” you can swap between characters.
  • What you build is what you get — the preview matches the final character exactly.
  • Roster tools to edit stats, spawn points and test a character’s voice on the spot.
13 / Combat

Physics Combat & Reactive AI

Every swing is a real collision — and the AI fights you for real or performs for the crowd.

Combat is genuinely physical: your weapon is really swinging, and if it meets the enemy’s blade before it reaches them, the blow is stopped — a true block, never a canned animation. A twin-stick option and a real off-hand slot add directional slashing and shields. And the AI knows the difference between a show and a fight: it stages dramatic clashes between characters, then turns into a real challenger the moment you pick up a controller.

  • Blocking is physical — weapon meets weapon, with knockback, sparks, parries, and punishing counters.
  • Twin-stick and a real off-hand — directional slashing plus a slot for a second weapon or a working shield.
  • Bare-handed fighting — jabs, crosses, kicks and roundhouses when you’re unarmed or lose an arm.
  • Two AI moods — a choreographed showman for AI-vs-AI drama, a sharp opponent for you.
  • Defense is defense — you block, you don’t dodge. A deliberate design rule.
14 / Weapons

The Arsenal

Six shell types, a wall of shotguns, chain flails, a reeling harpoon, and an RPG that levels the scenery.

Guns fire real, dodgeable projectiles you feed from ammo you switch on the fly, and six shot types transform every blast — buckshot, slug, incendiary, explosive, corrosive acid, and “brown.” Beyond the guns is a whole cabinet of hand-crafted weapons, each its own little toy, across a catalog of roughly 78 items.

  • Six shot types — each with its own damage, its own after-effect, and its own colored casing.
  • A deep gun roster — six shotguns, Uzi/AK/M16, pistols, a flamethrower, even guns that spray water, acid, oil or blood.
  • Bullets bury into the scenery and then detonate or set it on fire.
  • Exotic gear — a harpoon that spears a limb and reels the victim in, a tranquilizer rifle, a rocket launcher.
  • Toys with personality — chain flails and nunchucks, gas grenades and a gas mask, a landmine, a two-handed battle-axe, and a flaming mop.
15 / Carnage

Gore, Fire, Liquids & Status Effects

Twelve limbs with their own health bars, floors you can set on fire, and a town full of drunk, puking fighters.

Every character is a ragdoll with health in each limb — take off an arm and they drop their weapon, take off the head and they’re gone. Blood and worse spray across the floor. Explosions bloom into fire; floors pool with water, oil, acid and sewage that reflect, flow and react to each other; and anyone — you included — can end up on fire, drunk, dissolving in acid, or passed out.

  • Health in all 12 body parts — lose your legs and you can’t walk, lose an arm and you can’t hold a weapon.
  • Blood and mess that respects the stage, spreading toward you and squishing toward the back.
  • Fire that spreads — characters and props ignite, burn, and get doused by water.
  • Living chemistry — oil catches fire, acid eats craters and neutralizes in sewage, blood drifts down running water.
  • Four tiers of drunk and contagious filth — and every status effect works on the AI too, and survives scene changes.
16 / Companions

Party & Companions

Recruit a crew of souls — and carry them home when it goes wrong.

It’s a CRPG, so you don’t have to go it alone. Talk almost any of the ~250 into joining your party, and they’ll follow you, fight for you, and take direction — each still thinking for itself. Knock a companion out and you can sling them over your shoulder and carry them to safety, and your whole crew travels with you from place to place.

  • Recruit companions by talking them round — almost anyone in the cast can join you.
  • Followers fight at your side, take weapons and items you hand them, and keep their own personality.
  • Carry a downed ally across scenes instead of leaving them behind.
  • Your whole party comes with you through doorways — and gets hauled into court together if you’re arrested.
  • They’re still their own souls — loyalty isn’t a guarantee.
17 / Multiplayer

Couch Co-op, Up to Four

Grab up to three friends and share the chaos.

Beaver Valley is built for the couch. Up to four players can drop in together — each picks a fighter from the full roster and causes problems side by side, across the town, the arena, and the battlefield. More hands just means more mayhem, and friendly fire is very much on the table.

  • 1 to 4 players, chosen right from the character select.
  • Everyone picks their own character from the whole ~250-strong roster.
  • Play together across the town, the VS arena, and the war front.
  • The AI keeps challenging all of you at once — it turns competitive the moment humans join.
  • Friendly fire is on — your worst enemy is on the same couch.
18 / Medicine

The Hospital & Emergency Services

Get shot, get patched up, get billed — or get a cybernetic arm.

The Valley runs a working county hospital, staffed on its own schedule. Take enough damage and you can be treated, cured, or rebuilt — missing limbs replaced with plastic or full cybernetic ones, ailments and acid burns cured, casts applied that mend over time. Bleed out in the street and a 911 EMT can be dispatched to scrape you up. And when it’s too late, there’s an autopsy, a cremation, and an urn to take home.

  • Wound healing, ailment and acid-burn cures, and casts that mend over time.
  • Lost a limb? Replace it — plastic, or a full cybernetic upgrade.
  • Call 911 and an EMT is dispatched to collect the wounded.
  • Autopsy, cremation, and a keepsake urn for the ones who don’t make it.
  • Every charge billed through your debt — and the fire department answers emergencies on the same logic.
